# Phase 01 — Problem Definition: BRIEF

## Purpose

Narrow from a broad opportunity into a specific, well-framed problem worth solving. This phase answers: **"What exactly is the problem, who has it, and what does a good solution look like?"**

A well-defined problem constrains everything downstream — concept development, design decisions, trade-offs, pricing. Skipping or rushing this phase is the single most common reason physical products fail to find their market.

---

## Inputs Needed

- Phase 00 HANDOVER.md (or equivalent if Phase 00 was skipped)
- Any pre-existing knowledge about the user, their pain points, or the use context
- Founder/team assumptions — what do you believe to be true that hasn't been validated?

---

## Sub-Task Menu

### Core (recommended for all projects)

- [ ] **Problem statement drafting** — Write a clear, specific problem statement. What is the problem, who experiences it, in what context, and what are the consequences of it going unsolved?
- [ ] **User/customer deep-dive** — Go deeper than the Phase 00 sketch. Who is the primary user? Who is the buyer (if different)? What are their behaviours, motivations, frustrations?
- [ ] **Jobs-to-be-done mapping** — What functional, emotional, and social jobs is the user trying to get done? What does "success" look like from their perspective?
- [ ] **Current alternatives analysis** — How do people solve this problem today? What's good and bad about each alternative? Where is the dissatisfaction?
- [ ] **Assumption inventory** — List every assumption baked into the opportunity and problem framing. Flag which are validated, which are guesses, and which are critical to test.

### Conditional (select based on product type and situation)

- [ ] **Use context mapping** — Where, when, and how is this product used? Physical environment, social context, frequency, duration. Critical for hardware where form factor and durability depend on context.
- [ ] **Stakeholder mapping** — Beyond the end user — who else matters? Retailers, gift-givers, installers, parents, regulators, platform owners?
- [ ] **Pain severity & frequency scoring** — How painful is the problem and how often does it occur? A framework for prioritising which aspects of the problem matter most.
- [ ] **Willingness-to-pay framing** — Not pricing yet, but understanding: what is the user currently spending (in money, time, effort) on this problem? What would they give up?
- [ ] **Segmentation exploration** — Are there distinct user segments with different needs? Should the product target one segment narrowly or serve multiple?
- [ ] **Emotional/aspirational dimension** — Beyond function — what does the user want to feel? Status, joy, calm, confidence, belonging? Especially relevant for design-heavy and lifestyle products.
- [ ] **Gift & occasion mapping** — Is this a self-purchase or a gift? Tied to occasions, seasons, or life events? Affects packaging, pricing, and channel strategy.

### Optional / Deep Dive

- [ ] **Problem evolution timeline** — How has this problem changed over time? Is it getting worse, shifting, or being partially solved by new tech/trends?
- [ ] **Extreme user analysis** — Who experiences this problem most acutely? What can we learn from edge cases and power users?
- [ ] **Analogous problem study** — Are there problems in other domains that were solved in interesting ways that could inform this one?
- [ ] **Failure mode pre-mortem** — Imagine the product launched and failed. What's the most likely reason? Work backwards from failure to identify problem definition gaps.

---

## Done Criteria

This phase is complete when:

1. A clear, specific problem statement exists that the team believes in
2. The target user is understood well enough to make design decisions against
3. The jobs-to-be-done are mapped and prioritised
4. Key assumptions are inventoried and flagged for validation in Phase 02
5. The HANDOVER.md provides enough context for Research & Insight to be targeted and efficient

---

## Notes

- The problem statement will likely be revised after Phase 02 (Research & Insight). That's expected — this is the best articulation based on current knowledge.
- For products entering with strong existing insight (e.g., the founder IS the target user), this phase can be lighter but should still be done. Your blind spots are hardest to see when you're closest to the problem.
- The distinction between user and buyer matters enormously for physical products. A children's toy is used by a child but bought by a parent. A kitchen gadget is bought by one household member but used by everyone. A board game is bought by one person but played by a group.
